Twentieth Century: 1900 - 1999

Overview

  • Composers made new rules based on modernism.
  • Colour, gestures and algorithms: inspiration and grounding.
  • Rhythmic techniques, e.g. syncopation and changing tempo.
  • Atonal melodies, e.g. Schoenberg’s Pierrot Lunaire (1912).
  • Post-WWII, techniques branched out.
  • Pierre Boulez: control; serialism dictating dynamics, duration and pitch.
  • John Cage: freedom and chance.






Key Composer: Arnold Schoenberg (1874-1951)

Tonal early compositions, e.g. Verklärte Nacht (1899), although beginnings of harmonic innovation present. Contains an uncategorised inverted ninth chord; rejected by listeners. (Kuiper & Newlin, 1998). Dispensed with tonality in Drei Klavierstücke (1909). (ibid.) Twelve tone system: none of the tones have greater focus than another; no tonal centre, e.g. Fünf Klavierstücke (1923). (Lumen Learning). Hostility towards his approach to music; newspaper debates. (Fox, 2009). Gained appreciation after WWI, went on to teach in the USA. (Kuiper & Newlin, 1998).

Key Work: The Rite of Spring – Igor Stravinsky (1913)

A ballet that shocked its audience; shouting and arguments on opening night. (Hewett, 2016) Story of old pagan Russian ritual: young girl chosen for sacrifice, dances herself to death. (ClassicFM) Confrontational: relentless, pulsing, polymetric and additive rhythms. Dissonance; uncomfortable atmosphere. (Schwarm, 2013) Different to any previous ballet, as Stravinsky said: “Very little immediate tradition lies behind The Rite of Spring – and no theory.” (Service, 2013) Traditional and innovative: melodies from folk songs, although new to Parisian audience in 1913 (ibid.)
